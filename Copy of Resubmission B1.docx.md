| The MARGINS project aims at revolutionizing the field that concerns the history of the Jewish book by collecting and synthesizing both existing and new relevant data, and through the application of novel methods. Understanding the developments of the early modern period demands greater attention to the cultural and physical role played by printed books, and particularly their materiality. We will therefore examine three central elements of Hebrew print in the early modern period: (1) Fonts or typefaces; (2) Graphic elements including images, decorations and the layout of the page; (3) Paratexts (texts that accompany the main text of a book, such as front-pages, introductions, colophons, approbations etc.).  The expected output will be monographs, edited volumes, anthologies, an integrated platform for detection, classification and analysis of fonts and images, open access datasets, and a web-based system for the presentation and exploration of both data and metadata. The monographs and edited volumes will analyze the data in an interdisciplinary framework: history, art history, religious studies, sociology, linguistics, literature, and more. The anthologies of paratexts, in annotated digital and print editions, both in the original languages and in English translation, will supply a wide gamut of previously unstudied material for further investigation. |

# **Section a: Extended Synopsis of the scientific proposal**

We are living through a vast revolution concerning the place of the written and spoken word. The stunning advent of artificial intelligence has driven this home even more powerfully.  A crucial avenue for understanding this groundbreaking moment is the in-depth examination of the last great revolution of this kind, using the finest contemporary conceptual and technological tools available today.

The early modern book emerged through remarkably intense engagements with other forms of textuality and with communities of scholars and believers. The Hebrew book is no exception: it stands as a prime exemplar of the shift towards mass communication in early modern culture. Furthermore, it exemplifies the intersection between sacred and secular language—a key feature of modernization. 

The MARGINS project aims at revolutionizing the field of the history of the Jewish book by collecting and integrating existing and new data, and by applying novel methods in its study. This demands greater comprehension of the cultural and physical role of printed books, particularly their materiality. Three central elements of early modern Hebrew print will be examined: (1) fonts or typefaces; (2) graphic elements that include images, decorations and page layouts; and (3) paratexts (texts that accompany the main text of a book, such as front-pages, introductions, colophons, approbations etc.).

To date, these elements have largely escaped the systematic purview of scholars. This project will address the following fundamental questions regarding Jewish printed texts: 

1. What are the cultural streamlines, in time and place, of Jewish printing?   
2. What are the networks of the agents of Jewish printing?  
3. What is the Jewish paratextual discourse and what can we infer from it?

The history of the book has attracted growing scholarly attention, no doubt due to the impact of the current digital age. In recent decades, the transition to electronic media have heightened the cultural significance of the book as a material object. These material aspects emerged in the early modern period with the creation of printed books. While scholarly attention has addressed the text of books, this project will focus on the margins of the book and of printing culture.  

The history of the book is more developed in larger European cultures than in the field of Jewish studies. It already includes traditional scholarly outputs, introductions, textbooks, readers, and even guides for teaching the field. These studies do not usually mention Jewish books. This is due to the vast number of books in Latin characters, and no less to the difficulty in reading Hebrew and other Jewish languages. 

Advances in Jewish scientific bibliography in the 19th and 20th centuries laid the foundations for the modern listing of texts – manuscripts and printed books – that are the core of Jewish culture. But broader thinking about the meaning of the existence and production of books, and the people engaged in it has developed well beyond mere lists. Books hold an important place in all Abrahamic religions, which are also religions of the book., though the Jewish approach to books as sacred objects of worship and study is unique. The field of ‘Jewish Thought’, which focuses on the major works of Jewish thinkers, pays only cursory attention to how the availability of texts in printed form changed Jewish text culture. 

The dense intertextuality of Jewish texts requires reflection on the intellectual dimension crucial for comprehending the role of the book in Jewish culture. The establishment of the Jewish National and University Library in 1925 launched an institutional treatment of Jewish books. Distinguished librarians and scholars in Palestine, Europe, and the U.S. began to consolidate Jewish bibliography. Impressive initiatives included the Bibliography of the Hebrew Book Project, which records all Hebrew-letter books in print before 1960\. In the digital age, several pioneering databases were created:  The Friedberg Genizah Project, now housed at the National Library of Israel; *Ktiv: The International Collection of Digitized Hebrew Manuscripts*; the Responsa Project; Footprints (tracks the journeys of individual books); and Hebrewbooks, which has scanned more than 65,000 traditional volumes.

**The Current Stage of Research**  

*Fonts* 

Hebrew typeset letters were among the earliest created in Europe, second only to those prepared for Latin print. Like the Latin letters, they relied heavily on the local or regional scribal practices and were created within various Italian, Spanish, Ashkenazi (=German), and Mediterranean families. 

Despite important scholarly efforts undertaken in the past half-century, the study of Jewish fonts so far is very partial and inconsistent. To date, no attempt has been made to investigate the field in a systematic and comprehensive framework, as planned in this proposal.

Several steps in the workflow of font classification can be automated with relative ease, such as glyph parsing and printed letter recognition. Recent advances in automatic detection and classification of font types have been achieved using neural networks and transformer models. In Hebrew, efforts have been directed towards classification and are centered around the typology of handwritten scripts. 

We intend to define, and ultimately produce a detailed taxonomy for each of the Hebrew fonts used by printers prior to the nineteenth century. This will identify Jewish books printed with the same fonts, which will become extremely useful for answering questions about the many books and broadsides whose provenance is unknown.

*Graphical Elements and Images*

In contrast to various DH projects devoted to Christian print, only sparse attention has been paid to the graphic elements in Jewish print. In the 1980s, Chone Shmeruk examined the images that were printed in Yiddish books in the 17th century, and other recent studies on Hebrew printing are helpful and we intend to expand such research with our findings.

A database of the images and the graphic elements in Hebrew print will enhance our understanding of printed Jewish visual culture and will provide a basis for comparison to Christian visual culture. Our study of the graphic elements will also enable us to locate centers of printing.  

*Paratexts* 

A digital text corpus of paratexts will make it possible to discover new relationships among individuals involved in the book production industry, for example, to establish family relations between the various agents of bookmaking. It will aid in identifying the development of linguistic expressions and themes, as well as networks uniting agents of the book.  

Although the field of paratextual studies received a boost with the advent of digital methods for the representation, study and analysis of text, the study of Jewish paratexts is very much in its infancy. While the database of the bibliography of the Hebrew book currently contains 115,000 bibliographic records and more than 15,000 biographies, these are short and the paratexts are abbreviated.  

**Broadsides as a test case**

A test case for applying our findings will be a study dedicated to Jewish broadsides. Broadsides provide a unique perspective on everyday Jewish life. both as expressions of the community (wedding songs, riddles, wall calendars, internal community dispositions) and its relationship to the Christian or Muslim surroundings (regulations and laws). Jewish broadsides represent roughly ten percent of the total printed production of Jewish books. The main contributions thus far are the Valmadonna library catalogue (2015), and my own studies published since the 2020s. I have been collecting information about broadsides as part of a project funded by the Israel Science Foundation. 

The study of broadsides is incorporated in this project because they are a neglected yet critical ad hoc print product that should be considered together with the larger venture of book printing. Since most broadsides do not include indications of place and date, the intersection of fonts and images will make it possible to pin-point their origin. Because they are comparatively short and carry distinct and often exclamatory expressions, they reveal a visible connection between form and matter.   

The MARGINS project will utilize a corpus consisting of all books and broadsides printed in Hebrew fonts, from the beginning of Hebrew printing until the end of the 18th century. There are about 150 incunabula, about 10,000 titles in the 16th century, 20,000 in the 17th century, and 30,000 in the 18th century, so all together, about 60,000 books. About 7,000 of them are available with high-quality scans, in the National Library of Israel, in European libraries, and in the U.S. 

It is hoped that the intersection between the three trajectories will reveal hidden dimensions that are fundamental to the history of Jewish and western cultures of communication. It will become clear that the development of the early modern Jewish textual culture must be approached through the conjunction of the textual and the physical elements of the printed book.

**Methodology**

MARGINS studies two different aspects that together comprise books: the visual and the textual. Fonts and Graphic elements are of course visual, and paratexts are mostly textual. Because there has been comparatively little previous study of these elements in Jewish print culture, the analysis of each of them separately, and especially their combined analysis, would entail a breakthrough in the study of Jewish print culture, and early modern Jewish culture. 

Another important aspect of the methodology is that of dynamic modeling: We commence with known font families and types, the appearance or lack of frames in title pages, the appearance of designed empty space on the page, emblematic and graphic categories, conceptions about sub-genres of paratexts, or of the roles of actors in early modern book making. We begin by constructing a preliminary ontology and vocabularies, which will be subject to test, revision or confutation. Since many such preconceived categories were formed on the basis of research in the non-Jewish domains, or with no quantitative data-informed evidence, it is expected that the ensuing knowledge system will differ from the initial one.

The state of the art in the fields relevant to both paratexts and broadsides: digital text analysis, investigation and presentation, comprises a multi-stage process. Models for historic printed Hebrew are available in both Transkribus and eScriptorium and can be easily fine-tuned for specific text types. Layout — though not a research pillar on the same footing as fonts, images and paratexts — is the indispensable substrate that links them: zone and line segmentation determines what a transcription model sees as a haskama block, a marginal gloss, a title-page ornament frame, or a colophon. We will therefore adopt (and where necessary adapt for Hebrew-print specifics: right-to-left reading order, square-script marginalia around rabbinic-letter main text, chronogram-bearing title pages) the **SegmOnto** controlled vocabulary for page-layout zones and lines (https://segmonto.github.io/), so that layout annotations produced in our pipeline are interoperable with the wider HTR/OCR community and reusable across Transkribus and eScriptorium projects. The information contained in the texts is modeled and saved in a cloud-based database. 

The iteratively gathered data, modeled according to the gradually developing ontologies and vocabularies, will be gathered in an interactive web platform that will serve the project team. Towards the project’s conclusion the platform will be opened up to the research community to make use of the information that has been gathered and tools developed for further research projects. 

Paratexts will be a significant part of the datasets. We will strive to include all paratexts of the first hundred years of Jewish print, and later paratexts will feature in the iteratively growing sample. The broadsides, on the other hand, will serve as the first case study for testing and implementing the developing tools and methods for typology of fonts and graphic elements. 

The same principles of dynamic modeling and iterative work will guide this work: we will commence by linking the annotated entities to a set of authorities for all people, places and organizations in the early set and in the sample sets of later material, and continue by gradually enhancing both the tools of knowledge extraction and by expanding the prosopography of book makers and book making establishments, while constantly reassessing our categories of relations between them. Besides the printers, the lauded funding patrons, dedicators or dedicatees, the lists of institutions and individuals will be complemented through the work on fonts and graphic elements classification. 

Towards the conclusion of the project, the platform will be made available to the public. Categories will be used as facets and filters for inspecting and selecting the data, and a timeline and map interface will be included to enable spatio-temporal exploration of the studied phenomenon, and a full-text search functionality will facilitate the retrieval of phrases, citations etc.  

**Work Plan**

## Year 1 will be dedicated to setting up the infrastructure, initial sample collection and digital tool runs, and the following tasks:   {#year-1-will-be-dedicated-to-setting-up-the-infrastructure,-initial-sample-collection-and-digital-tool-runs,-and-the-following-tasks:}

* Team recruitment, work group assignments, setting up bi-weekly reading sessions devoted to the study of fonts and images, reading selected paratexts and broadsides, and pooling the diverse linguistic and intellectual resources of the team.

* Collecting initial samples of book pages for font analysis and of relevant paratexts, and using careful close reading, by existing annotation tools, to identify and describe fonts and graphical elements, analyze the paratexts date the different exemplars. 

* Assembling thesauri of known entities such as people, printing establishments and locations.

* Begin work on paratext OCR and structure encoding in XML-TEI, with page-layout segmentation aligned to the SegmOnto vocabulary (adapted for Hebrew-print zone types where needed) so that zone/line annotations are interoperable across Transkribus and eScriptorium and reusable downstream.

* Towards the end of the first year, we will start running pre-trained digital tools for image recognition, text analysis, image identification and classification, and knowledge extraction. 

## Year 2  {#year-2}

* The project’s database, which will be built as a cloud-based repository for the collected data in its various forms, will include font related tables, graphical elements related tables, connections to external or local document sources and other information. 

* The development of a font recognition tool, will comprise a high resolution scanner and will run the analysis with models trained on previous datasets.  . 

* The creation of a classification tool and a database tables for graphical elements.

## Year 3 will focus on the project’s digital platforms will start to take shape with integrated web based tools and initial analysis of broadsides as a test case. {#year-3-will-focus-on-the-project’s-digital-platforms-will-start-to-take-shape-with-integrated-web-based-tools-and-initial-analysis-of-broadsides-as-a-test-case.}

* Font recognition will be implemented as a web based tool and integrated with the font database. 

* A similar graphical element classification tool will be implemented on the web.

* The project’s document database will be set up as an XML database, to hold the annotated encoded paratexts and annotated broadsides. The broadsides research team will start working on broadside analysis, which will be based on the initial work on text and paratexts.  

## Years 4 and 5  {#years-4-and-5}

The concluding years of the project will be dedicated to integration and refinement of the project’s results, and to the dissemination of those results through the data frontend as well through the publication of anthologies and monographs. The project’s data presentation application will be implemented via a suitable open-source platform. Serving as a frontend to the project’s databases, it will provide a catalogue of printed objects capable of noting different copies at different locations with different features, such as preservation status, digital accessibility, content description, cataloguing depictions, and analysis of the fonts employed. A final conference will be held during the last year and will demonstrate the results of the research and propose new perspectives for future scholarship.

## **The Team** {#the-team}

The project will consist of an administrative coordinator and two teams: Scientific and Technological, that will be in constant collaboration. The research group will include three outstanding post-doc students, two scientific workers and a data scientist, who will be responsible for the technical elements of the research. The structure of the scientific team, headed by the PI, will correspond to the three main research subjects and one devoted to broadsides. The digital aspects of the work will be performed and coordinated by DHSS Hub of the Open University of Israel.  

**Impact and Dissemination of Results** 

The results of the project will be published in open-access databases, two anthologies containing images and paratexts, and five printed and open-access digital monographs, based on the work of the PI and the post-doctoral students. The proceedings of the conference at the conclusion of the project will be published in leading journals in the fields of book history and the bibliography of the Hebrew book, and in Jewish studies journals. “Consultations” will be proposed to the annual meetings of the Society of the History of Reading, Authorship, and Print (SHARP); to the Renaissance Society of America (RSA); and to the World Congress of Jewish Studies. The PI will also author a programmatic article in a leading journal in the field and will initiate discussion on various themes of the project at the national forums for history and Jewish studies. A website will be created for publishing the ongoing progress, advertising the conferences, and as a platform for scholarly and public discussion.

**A short narrative description** 

Throughout my academic career, I have been deeply fascinated by cultural marginality and its capacity to illuminate the core of a society. My research has consistently focused on texts that reside at the boundaries of Jewish culture, exploring how these peripheral works reflect and influence the center. My initial work delved into the peripheral yet significant genre of Jewish death-bed literature, which opened the door to broader studies in Jewish book history, personal writings, magic, Jewish mysticism, and secularization. In the course of my research, I have coined several theoretical concepts, such as "library conception" (which defines how libraries are conceived within specific historical and cultural contexts) and "textual intimacy" (the reciprocal relationship between readers and the text), which have contributed to our understanding of these areas. MARGINS represents the culmination of my scholarly journey—a synthesis of my enduring fascination with books and Jewish culture, and a deep exploration of the margins that shape and define the cultural center. Additionally, this research promises to offer innovative methodologies and insights that will significantly advance the study of early modern Jewish book culture.
